What keeps us ticking?

What forms the basis of learned skills? How do cells in the brain self-organize to retain information, and how is this information properly maintined?

Nearly all living creatures maintain learned motor skills over long time-scalesâ€“for days, years or even decades. However, little is known about the mechanistic basis skill learning and maintnence.

I develop tools for longitudinally monitoring and manipulating large populations of cells in awake freely behaving animals, and deploy these tools in creative ways. I'm interested studying the plasticity mechanisms that allow for stable network output in the face of rapidly fluctuating internal and external conditions. I hope to discover single neuron rules that underlie sensory-motor learning and memory stability.

In my graduate work in the Gardner Lab, I designed neural interfaces to ask questions in one of the most stable of all animal behaviors: birdsong. The neural circuits that underly song behavior are well defined, extensively studied, and in key respects homologous to the cortico-basal ganglia circuits underlying sensory-motor learning in mammals. For this work, the key value of the songbird is the stability of its natural motor behavior. A songbird can sing the same learned song with great precision for years. My findings revealed that the learned behaviors can be encoded on two distinct levels- a mesoscopic level that is both locally organized and highly stable, and a microscopic level in which single neurons change their activity, influenced by the recent history of reward. These changes are integrated offline durring periods of sleep.

As a postdoctoral fellow in the Carmena Lab, I'm developing experiments using calcium imaging based Brain Machine Interfaces (BMIs). I'm using a reductionist approach where behavioral output is simply a specific spatiotemporal activity pattern produced in experimenter defined neurons. To precisely constrain neural activity, I exploit a neuron-centric framework in which the relevant output neurons and rewarded activity patterns are defined a priori. This approach is ideal because it reduces the redundancy and degeneracy that is broadly found in motor behavior, and it can explicitly constrain both the intrinsic jitter in motor production and the potential variability of performance evaluation. These BMI techniques leverage the latest advances in molecular and optical engineering to provide unprecedented access to large populations of genetically identified neurons across timescales relevant to learning.

Current Projects

These are the projects I'm actively working on.

Motor Stability and Error Coding

Memories and motor skills can be maintained for decades, but the biological basis of memory persistence remains largely unknown. it is clear that significant homeostatic events act across days to fine tune and stabilize behavioral output, but it remains unclear how these continual changes assist in acquiring and maintaining learned skills. How might continually dynamic circuitry impart both skill robustness and flexibility throughout life? I'm using a BMI framework to answer long standing questions about the nature of memory formation and stability for neural skills on two scales: the local cortical microcircuit and of the anatomically distributed cortical network to define the single-unit plasticity mechanisms that result in the balance of both the rapid acquisition and long-term stability of learned skills.

Engineering new tools for optical recording and stimulation

Optical recording of neural activity in the brains of behaving animals has become an essential method in systems neuroscience. Through the use of genetically encoded calcium indicators, the principles of learning can be studied in large ensembles of cells at cellular resolution. I'm developoing new tools for monitoring and perturbing neural activity in discrete, user-defined cell populations.

Neuroprosthetic Learning

How do individual neruons self-assemble into netowrks that are capeable of complex computations? What are the fundimental features of these networks? To address this, I'm developing all optical, closed-loop operant conditioning paradigms that enable an explicit, arbitrary mapping between neuronal activity, behavioral output, and reward, to provide a powerful approach for studying sensorimotor learning,

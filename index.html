What keeps us ticking?

What forms the basis of memory stability? How do cells in the brain self-organize to retain information, and how is this information properly maintined?

Nearly all living creatures maintain learned motor skills over long time-scalesâ€“for days, years or even decades. However, little is known about the mechanistic basis skill learning and maintnence .

I develop tools for longitudinally monitoring and manipulating large populations of cells in awake freely behaving animals, and deploy these tools in creative ways. I'm interested studying the plasticity mechanisms that allow for stable network output in the face of rapidly fluctuating internal and external conditions. I hope to discover single neuron rules that underlie sensory-motor learning and memory stability.

In the Gardner Lab, I designed neural interfaces to ask questions in one of the most stable of all animal behaviors: birdsong. The neural circuits that underly song behavior are well defined, extensively studied, and in key respects homologous to the cortico-basal ganglia circuits underlying sensory-motor learning in mammals. For this work, the key value of the songbird is the stability of its natural motor behavior. A songbird can sing the same learned song with great precision for years. My findings revealed that the learned behaviors can be encoded on two distinct levels- a mesoscopic level that is highly stable, and a microscopic level in which single neurons change their activity, influenced by the recent history of reward. These changes are integrated offline durring periods of sleep.

Current Projects

These are the project's I'm actively working on.

Motor Stability and Error Coding

Memories and motor skills can be maintained for decades, but the biological basis of memory persistence remains largely unknown. The zebra finch, for example, sings a highly stereotyped song that is stable for years, but the underlying neural activity has not been observed for more than a single day. Using Calcium imagign, I'm trying to uncover the mechanisms of memory persistacne by observing how individual neurons respond to percieved performance errors.

Engineering new tools for optical recording and stimulation

Optical recording of neural activity in the brains of behaving animals has become an essential method in systems neuroscience. Through the use of genetically encoded calcium indicators, the principles of learning can be studied in large ensembles of cells at cellular resolution. I'm developoing new tools for monitoring and perturbing neural activity in discrete, user-defined cell populations.
